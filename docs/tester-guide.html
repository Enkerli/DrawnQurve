Let's just play

What you need

  • An iPad (any model, iPadOS 16+)
  • A synth app — AudioKit Synth One is free on the App Store
  • DrawnQurve (you got it via TestFlight)

That's the whole setup. No cables, no routing, no host app. DrawnQurve talks directly to your synth through an invisible connection.

Your first playflow

1

Open your synth app (AudioKit Synth One).

Tap a key on screen — you should hear sound. Good. Leave it running.

2

Switch to DrawnQurve.

You'll see a big empty canvas. That's where you draw.

3

Draw a curve. Drag your finger across the canvas — any shape. A swoop, a zigzag, a mountain range. Whatever feels right.

4

Tap Play (top left). Your curve is now looping, and the synth should be responding. You're musicking.

What you hear depends on the mode DrawnQurve is in. By default it's in Note mode — the height of your curve becomes pitch. High points = high notes, low points = low notes. The curve loops, and you hear a melody. (Probably a fast glissando — that's normal! Keep reading.)

Heads up: DrawnQurve has its own play button (top left). If you're also using GarageBand, its play button controls GarageBand's transport — not DrawnQurve's. They're independent.

What's a qurve?

Your first drawing probably produced a rapid stream of notes — a glissando. That's because a freehand curve is smooth, and smooth means every tiny movement becomes a different note.

Now try this: tap one of the lock icons next to the grid. The Y-lock snaps your curve's output to the grid lines. Instead of sliding between every possible value, it jumps between grid levels. What was a smooth curve is now a step pattern.

That's a qurve. Part curve, part staircase. The Q is for quantize — snapping to a grid. And it changes everything about how your drawing sounds.

The a-ha moment

Draw any shape. Listen to the glissando. Now tap Y-lock. Same shape, completely different feel. Add X-lock (snaps the timing to the grid) and suddenly your freehand scribble is a crisp rhythmic pattern. You're drawing step sequences.

This is the moment where drawn curves become qurves — and where DrawnQurve starts to feel like a musical instrument.

More things to explore

  • Draw again while it's playing — the new curve replaces the old one instantly
  • Speed — the slider in the top bar makes the loop faster or slower
  • Direction — the three-way control (top left, next to Play) lets you play forward, reverse, or ping-pong
  • The grid — the small +/− buttons on the edges of the canvas change how many grid divisions there are. More divisions = finer steps
  • Scales — tap the note badge on the canvas to open the scale browser. Pick a scale and your notes snap to it

#MIDIsplainer

If MIDI feels confusing, that's not on you. It's a standard from 1983 that has been explained badly for decades. You're not a "MIDIot" — that's a word some people use, and it's unfair. MIDI is genuinely confusing, and the best way to understand it is to play with it.

That's exactly what DrawnQurve lets you do.

#MIDIsplainer — the 30-second version: MIDI is how music apps and instruments talk to each other. It doesn't carry sound — it carries instructions. "Play this note." "Bend the pitch." "Turn this knob to 73." A synth receives those instructions and makes sound from them.

DrawnQurve draws those instructions. You trace a shape, and it becomes a looping stream of MIDI messages that a synth interprets as music. Different modes send different kinds of messages:

  • Note mode — your curve's height becomes pitch. You're drawing a melody.
  • CC mode — "continuous control." Like drawing the movement of a knob or slider on someone else's synth.
  • Pitch Bend — bends notes up and down, like a guitar whammy bar.
  • Pressure — aftertouch. On synths that support it, it's like varying how hard you press a key after it's already down.

You don't need to memorize any of this. Just start drawing and listen to what happens. The understanding follows from the experience.

Setup

Standalone (simplest)

DrawnQurve creates a virtual MIDI source port called "DrawnQurve". Any MIDI-aware app on your iPad will see it as a source automatically. Open a synth, open DrawnQurve, draw, play. Done.

  • AudioKit Synth One — free, responds to Note/CC/PB
  • GarageBand — free, first-party. Create an Instrument track (Keyboard, Strings, whatever you like). DrawnQurve's virtual port feeds it directly.
GarageBand notes: (1) Background audio must be enabled manually: iPad Settings → GarageBand → "Run in Background." Without this, GarageBand stops receiving MIDI when it's not the foreground app. (2) GarageBand's play button controls GarageBand's transport, not DrawnQurve's. They're separate — DrawnQurve has its own Play in the top-left corner.

The MIDI Out button in the toolbar opens a picker where you can:

  • Toggle the virtual port on/off (for when you don't want DrawnQurve broadcasting to every synth)
  • Add a direct target device (sends to that device in addition to the virtual port)

AUv3 host (advanced)

DrawnQurve is an AUv3 MIDI Effect (aumi). Load it as a MIDI FX plugin in any compatible host:

  • AUM — add as MIDI FX on a channel strip, route output to an instrument
  • Logic for iPad — insert on a Software Instrument track as MIDI FX
  • Loopy Pro — add as a MIDI FX node, route to a synth node
  • Audiobus 3 — place in the MIDI FX slot between source and destination

What's a qurve?

A freehand curve produces smooth, continuous output — every micro-movement becomes a slightly different value. In Note mode, that means rapid glissandi. In CC mode, it's a wobbly knob.

Tap a lock button to quantize the output. Y-lock snaps values to grid levels. X-lock snaps timing to grid columns. With both on, your fluid gesture becomes a crisp step pattern — part curve, part staircase. That's a qurve.

The grid density (+/− buttons) controls how many steps you get. More divisions = finer resolution. Fewer = bold, blocky jumps. In sync mode, the beat presets (♩ ♪ ♬) give those steps rhythmic meaning.

Features at a glance

  • 4 lanes — each with its own curve, MIDI routing (Note/CC/PB/Pressure), and loop settings
  • X/Y quantize — snap time and value to grid divisions. This is the curve → qurve transformation
  • Beat presets (sync mode) — step subdivisions (♬ ♪ ♩) and loop durations (1–32 beats)
  • Y presets — octave/dozen/PB range and step size, mode-dependent
  • Scale quantization — Note mode output snapped to a chosen scale + root (musical zone, bottom of screen)
  • Host sync — lock loop speed to host BPM (sync button, top bar)
  • Per-lane direction & speed — each lane can run forward/reverse/ping-pong at its own rate
  • Legato mode — Note mode: new note starts before old note ends (overlapping ties)

What we're testing

The core question: does DrawnQurve enable satisfying playflows?

A "playflow" is the full arc: drawing a curve, hearing it loop, refining it into something that makes you want to keep going. We're testing whether that sequence feels fluid, rewarding, and worth coming back to.

Things to try

  1. Draw a slow, simple shape. Play it. Does it feel immediate?
  2. Draw something fast and complex. Does the result surprise you?
  3. Turn on Y-lock (quantize). Draw the same shape. Different feel?
  4. Turn on X-lock too. Now you're drawing step sequences — qurves.
  5. Open the scale browser (tap the note badge on the canvas). Pick a scale. Draw again. Sound more musical?
  6. Add a second lane. Set it to CC mode. Now you're modulating and playing notes.
  7. Try sync mode (top bar). Set the duration to 4 beats. The step presets (♩ ♪ ♬) now mean something rhythmic.

Known issues & limitations

  • Touch targets are likely too small on many screens. This is a known issue we're actively sizing. Please report which controls are hardest to tap.
  • No cross-app sync. Host sync works within AUv3 hosts, but standalone DrawnQurve can't sync its transport to GarageBand's tempo or transport. Ableton Link is on the roadmap.
  • GarageBand background audio must be enabled manually in iPad Settings → GarageBand → "Run in Background."
  • DrawnQurve's Play ≠ GarageBand's Play. Each app has its own transport. DrawnQurve's is in the top-left corner of the app.
  • CoreMIDI errors in console (372, 388, etc.) may appear when switching between apps. These are transient and don't affect playback.
  • Console noise — "CA Event launch measurement" and "Tracking element window" warnings in Xcode are benign iOS system artefacts, not DrawnQurve bugs.

About DrawnQurve

DrawnQurve is a MIDI looper for iPad. You draw a shape; it loops as MIDI. Notes, continuous control, pitch bend, aftertouch — whatever you route it to. It's a tool for musicking: for anyone involved in music, not just musicians. Playing with music, not just playing music.

This is an alpha. Things will change. Your experience right now shapes what this becomes. Thank you for drawing with us.
